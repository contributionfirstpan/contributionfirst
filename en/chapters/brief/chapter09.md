## The Core Question: What Is Freedom?

### A Seemingly Simple Question

**Everyone says they want "freedom":**
- "Freedom and democracy" (political slogan)
- "Free market" (economic ideology)
- "Live freely" (personal aspiration)

**But what exactly is freedom?**

---

### Three Common Misunderstandings

**Misunderstanding 1: Freedom = No Constraints**
> "I want to do whatever I want, that's freedom!"

**Problem:**
- Your freedom to kill violates another's freedom to live
- Absolute freedom = No freedom (chaos)

---

**Misunderstanding 2: Freedom = Theoretical Rights**
> "The law says I'm free, so I'm free!"

**Problem:**
- Poor person: "I'm free to become a doctor"
- Reality: Cannot afford medical school
- Having rights â‰  Having capability

---

**Misunderstanding 3: Freedom = Following Desires**
> "I want to eat, so I eat; I want to buy, so I buy. That's freedom!"

**Problem:**
- Desires may be manipulated (advertising)
- Desires may be harmful (addiction)
- Enslaved by desires â‰  True freedom

## Isaiah Berlin's Framework: Two Concepts of Liberty

### Negative Liberty: Freedom From

**Definition:**
> "Am I prevented from doing X by anyone?"

**Core Concept:**
- Not being interfered with
- Not being coerced
- Not being constrained

**Examples:**
- Freedom of speech: No one forbids you from speaking
- Freedom of movement: No one prevents you from traveling
- Freedom of belief: No one forces you to believe

**Historical Achievement:**
- Enlightenment's great contribution
- Breaking theocratic and feudal control
- Individual autonomy

---

### Positive Liberty: Freedom To

**Definition:**
> "Can I actually do X? Do I have the resources, capability, and opportunity?"

**Core Concept:**
- Having capability to realize will
- Having resources and conditions
- Having actual choices

**Examples:**
- Freedom of education: Not just "not prevented from learning"
  - But actually having schools, teachers, books
  - Having time and resources
- Freedom of entrepreneurship: Not just "not prevented from starting business"
  - But having capital, knowledge, network
  - Having failure tolerance

**Historical Development:**
- 20th-century social rights movement
- Welfare state emergence
- Recognition that "formal freedom" insufficient

---

### Comparison Table

| Dimension | Negative Liberty | Positive Liberty |
|-----------|------------------|------------------|
| Core Question | Am I prevented? | Can I do it? |
| Focus | Removing obstacles | Providing conditions |
| Example | Not forbidden to speak | Having platform and audience |
| Historical Era | 18th-19th centuries (Enlightenment) | 20th century (Welfare state) |
| Representative | John Locke, John Stuart Mill | T.H. Green, Amartya Sen |

## Freedom's Boundary: The Non-Violence Principle

### Why Need Boundaries?

**Absolute Freedom Paradox:**
```

    â†“

    â†“

    â†“
```

**Conclusion:**
> One person's freedom cannot violate another's freedom.

### Three Types of Violence

#### 1. Direct Violence

**Physical harm:**
- Assault, murder, rape
- Property destruction (robbery, arson)
- Clear, unambiguous

**This is prohibited.**

---

#### 2. Structural Violence

**Exploitation and oppression:**
- Forced labor (sweatshops, slavery)
- Monopoly (controlling survival resources)
- Systemic discrimination (institutional racism, sexism)

**More hidden, but equally serious.**

**This is prohibited.**

---

#### 3. Ecological Violence

**Environmental destruction:**
- Severe pollution (endangering others' health)
- Species extinction (destroying diversity)
- Climate change (endangering future generations)

**Destroying future generations' freedom.**

**This is prohibited.**
